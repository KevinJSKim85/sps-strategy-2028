## 2. Core Principle: Keep Only Visible Impact

All activities in this strategy are evaluated by one criterion:

"Will it remain at school after graduation? Can other people see it? Can they hear it?"

Every year, there are students with high grades and awards. Very few leave something behind at SPS even after they are gone. This difference is what separates a "memorable applicant" from a "strong-on-paper applicant" in Top 20 admissions.

### 2.1 Use AI as a Tool

The biggest keyword of this era is AI. Do not list it as an "interest" only -- use it to build something real and deploy it at school.

Concrete project examples:

- Classics + AI: Build an AI Latin practice tool for SPS Classics classes. A web app that provides grammar analysis + translation feedback when students input Latin sentences. Co-develop with SPS Latin teacher Ryan Samuels and deploy in actual classes. -> Visible outcome: "My school is using a tool I built."
- Math + AI: Build a problem recommendation/solution analysis system for SPS Mathematics Society competition prep, or an AI math tutor for SPS students. -> Demonstrates both math strength and technical implementation.
- Research + AI: Apply AI/ML methods to ASEP research. Automate data analysis and accelerate simulation. -> Research productivity itself changes. Publication speed increases.
- Principle: Build tools that are actually installed/deployed in a school library, department, or administrative system. Code is on GitHub, users exist, and feedback exists. This is evidence of "built something for my school."

### 2.2 Research Is Not One or Two Papers

In the AI era, it is no longer unusual to produce papers weekly. Instead of chasing one "masterpiece," produce visible outputs consistently.

- Summer of 11th grade: ASEP Externship research -> 1 JEI paper
- During 11th grade semesters: Independent research or AI project -> arXiv preprint or additional paper
- 12th grade: Capstone + ISP -> additional publication or symposium presentation
- Presentations: Join every available presentation opportunity such as NESS STEM Symposium, SPS in-school poster sessions, and conferences near Dartmouth. Writing papers matters, but presenting in public creates "audible" impact.

### 2.3 Service/Contribution = Visible Deliverables

Time-log volunteering ("100 volunteer hours") has little value. Do only contributions with visible outcomes, as below.

- Build and give: Develop and donate digital learning tools to the SPS Classics department. If Ryan Samuels (Classical Honors lead) uses them in class, recommendation letters can explicitly state that this student built tools for the department.
- Build a system: Systematize peer tutoring in SPS Math Society. Not just tutoring -- build a curriculum and track participant count and outcomes. -> Impact can be shown numerically.
- Leave records: Present research/projects in Chapel Talk. It is publicly archived in the SPS website's "Heard in Chapel." -> A record that anyone can hear/access.

This principle applies to every activity in this document. Competition awards, paper publications, school activities -- all must produce outcomes people can see or hear. If not, do not do it.

## 3. Testing Strategy

### 3.1 SAT + ACT: Take Both

Most Top 20 universities accept both SAT and ACT. If you take both, you can submit only the higher score (Score Choice). High return for the effort and cost.

SAT target: 1530+ (competitive at HYPSM level). Math section 790-800 is required.  
ACT target: 35+ composite. Aim for 36 in Math/Science.

Timeline: Prepare ACT in summer of 10th grade -> first ACT in September. Take SAT in spring of 10th grade or fall of 11th grade. Having scores for both is advantageous. Retake whichever test is higher once more. Secure final scores by end of 11th grade.

### 3.2 TOEFL: You Do Not Need It

U.S. permanent resident + 3 years at SPS = TOEFL is waived at almost all Top 20 universities. Permanent residents are typically classified as domestic applicants, and TOEFL requirements often do not apply.

Conclusion: Do not take TOEFL. Use that time elsewhere.

Exception check required: Northwestern and Carnegie Mellon have policies that may require TOEFL based on "native language." Even with permanent residency, if native language is Korean, this may apply, so contact admissions directly if applying to these schools and confirm PR waiver eligibility. Brown marks it as "highly recommended," so submission is recommended, but waiver is still likely for permanent residents.

## 6. Science Fair -> ISEF Track

The Science Fair -> ISEF (International Science and Engineering Fair) path is one of the strongest STEM credentials in college admissions. But regional structures differ completely, so understanding the realistic NH-based path for SPS students is critical.

### 6.1 NH Reality: Thin Infrastructure but Opportunity

Key fact: New Hampshire has its own ISEF-affiliated fair (New Hampshire state science fair). It is not a combined regional competition with other states (e.g., Massachusetts, Connecticut). But NH is a small state, so only about 2-6 projects are sent to ISEF each year.

What this means: Fewer participants = fierce competition but low absolute numbers. Compared with California (30+ projects advancing), far fewer students are competing. With a strong project, ISEF advancement is realistically possible.

Caution: NH State Science Fair does not have a stable website. This is common in small states. Contact Society for Science directly (isef@societyforscience.org) to confirm NH fair director contact and dates.

### 6.2 ISEF Advancement Path (NH -> ISEF)

The path from NH to ISEF is simpler than in large states:

Path: SPS internal research -> NH State Science & Engineering Fair (3-4월) -> Regeneron ISEF (5월)

There is no complex school -> regional -> state sequence like in large states. NH State Fair itself is the ISEF qualifier.

### 6.4 Regeneron STS -- Separate Path (Most Realistic)

Regeneron Science Talent Search (STS) is entirely separate from ISEF.

STS does not require Science Fair qualification. You submit a research paper + full application directly. It is the most prestigious U.S. high school science competition (started 1942), and for SPS students it is often more realistic and accessible than NH State Fair.

Meaning of STS Top 300 Scholar: 300 selected from 2,500 nationwide. Scholar status alone is a very strong admissions signal. Top 40 Finalist is often a decisive factor for MIT/Harvard/Caltech-level admissions.

### 6.10 Writing Strategy -- Show Only Two Extremes

Writing outputs are split into two categories. There is no middle.

### 6.11 Research Paper Topics: AI + Classical Studies

To align with intended Classical Studies major applications, below are five research topics where AI/computational methods make real contributions to Classics. The goal is not "I used AI" but "a tool/discovery classicists can cite and use." Suitable for ISEF Computational & Statistical Sciences or Systems Software categories.

#### AI + Classical Studies Research Topics (5)

| Topic | In Simple Terms | Data/Methods | Contribution to Classics |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1. AI restoration of damaged Latin poetry with meter constraints | Epic Latin lines follow strict rhythm rules.<br>When AI fills missing text, it should restore not just "plausible sentences" but lines that match ancient poetic meter.<br>Output is a restoration tool researchers can directly use for damaged texts. | Perseus + Open Greek & Latin corpora.<br>Benchmark by artificially deleting words and restoring.<br>Meter (scansion)-based reranking system.<br>Human evaluation + automatic evaluation. | High feasibility.<br>Directly usable for textual criticism of damaged texts.<br>Citable format for classicists. |
| 2. Latin poetry "citation detector" -- explainable AI | Roman poets often transform earlier works in subtle ways.<br>Humans cannot scale this manually, and existing AI usually says only "similar."
This tool explains why (rare words, syntax, metrical position) and gives calibrated confidence.<br>Enables systematic discovery of influence relationships. | Tesserae (public intertext DB) + full Perseus text.<br>Hybrid of lemma n-gram + dense embeddings.<br>Explanation module + probability calibration. | Overcomes limits of existing tools (e.g., Tesserae).<br>Evidence-based similarity explanations are citable in scholarship.<br>Medium feasibility. |
| 3. Dating/regional inference of Latin inscriptions from phrase patterns | Estimating "when and where" Roman inscriptions originated is central in research.<br>Inscriptions contain linguistic clues.<br>Model predicts time/region from phrasing, orthography, abbreviations, and extracts historical patterns via maps/timelines. | Epigraphic Database Heidelberg (EDH) + EAGLE/EDCS public data.<br>Character n-gram + formulaic template features.<br>Interpretable classifier with SHAP. | High feasibility.<br>Direct contribution to epigraphy core tasks.<br>Date/region phrase maps become reference material for other scholars. |
| 4. Appendix Vergiliana authorship attribution with genre control | Whether Appendix Vergiliana is authentic Vergil has been debated for centuries.<br>This research evaluates probabilistically using meter patterns, function words, and syntax -- not intuition.<br>Controls for genre effects and provides evidence for why. | Appendix Vergiliana + confirmed Vergil corpus + contemporaries (Ovid, Propertius, etc.)<br>Perseus/Open Greek & Latin.<br>Bayesian ensemble + cross-validation. | Medium feasibility.<br>Targets a real unresolved Classics question.<br>Uncertainty quantification + genre control gives citable scholarly evidence. |
| 5. Gold-standard benchmark for Latin morphological analysis | Latin forms are often ambiguous across grammar/meaning.<br>Current tools frequently fail, distorting downstream statistics/search.
Create manually labeled difficult examples, evaluate existing tools fairly, and propose improved models.<br>This dataset can become field infrastructure. | Manually annotate thousands of difficult ambiguous forms from Perseus (poetry) + EDH (inscriptions).<br>Benchmark CLTK taggers + train lightweight classifier.<br>Release dataset + evaluation code. | High feasibility.<br>Foundational infrastructure for all computational Latin research.<br>Likely to be highly cited long term. |

Top recommendation #1: AI restoration of damaged Latin poetry (#1). High feasibility. Clear constraint structure through meter rules. Output tool is directly useful for classicists. A Latin-version narrative of DeepMind Ithaca -- very strong for college admissions.

Top recommendation #2: Dating/regional inference of Latin inscriptions (#3). Public data available (EDH). Interpretable model. Visible output as date/region phrase maps. Directly useful reference resource in epigraphy.

Top recommendation #3: Morphology Gold Standard benchmark (#5). Highest feasibility (manual annotation + benchmarking). Foundational infrastructure for the field -> strongest long-run citation potential.

Core resources:

- Tool: CLTK (Classical Language Toolkit): cltk.org -- Python NLP for Latin/Greek
- Corpus: Perseus Digital Library: perseus.tufts.edu -- full Latin/Greek texts for free
- Inscriptions: Epigraphic Database Heidelberg: edh.uni-heidelberg.de -- Roman inscription DB
- Intertext: Tesserae: tesserae.caset.buffalo.edu -- intertext detection tools/data
- Prior paper: Sommerschield et al. 2023: aclanthology.org/2023.cl-3.5/ -- ML for ancient languages survey

"I am drawn to systems of precise and beautiful rules -- whether that system is the grammar of the dead languages that built Western civilization, or the abstract structures of modern mathematics. Both demand the same kind of mind."

This is a rare, logical, and memorable intellectual identity. Skiing adds a third dimension: physical discipline, international competition, and persistence.
